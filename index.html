Humans Vs. Machines: An AI Odyssey

By Christopher Watkins

March 10,2016

***Breaking news: AlphaGo has won the first two matches!*** In this, third in our series on the epic go matches being played Between AlphaGo (Google artificial intelligence software) and Lee Se-Dol (Current Go World Champion), we look at the history of Humans vs. Mchines, and the innovations that have led to this incredible moment in time.

For as long as humans have built things, we've wrestle with the implications of what we've built. in many cases, these philosophical and ethical wrestlings have made for great drama-think Frankenstein,or 2001: A space Odyssey. Often, the hypotheticalscenarios we envision come remarkably close to true, and the discoveries we've made in the field of Artificial Intelligence and Machine learnig make clear that a "computer with a mind of its own" is going to take over the world not such a fantastic thing to imagine any longer.

The Thriump Of Deep Blue

Perhaps this is why we are so captivated by humans vs. machines competitions, because the idea of being ove come by that which we've created speaks to something very deep within our collective conciouness. When IBM's Deep Blue faced off against Garry Kasparov1, the event resulted in more than three billion impressions around the world, and when IBM's later creation Watson challanged the champions on Jeopardy, millions of viewers were glued to the proceedings.

DeepMind's AlphaGo

Taking pllace right now, there is an event that, while not likely to scale the same media heights, may in fact have far greater implications when it comes to the future of "intelligent" machines. On march 9, in Seoul, South Korea, a computing system knowns as AlphaGo (built by researchers at DeepMind-a Google Artificial Intelligence lab) began a five game match against Lee Se-Dol, one of the very best players in the world at the ancient game of Go.

Why is this so significant?

The game of Go has long been viewed as the most challenging of classic games for artificial intelligence due to its enormous search space and the difficult of evaluating board positions and moves.

Put another way, winning at Go is a kind of Holy Grails for those who strive to create machines that can "think" on their own, because success at this uniquely complex game seems to require something more than just skill, knowledge, and experience. It requires intuition. Feel. Style. Characteristics we associate with humans, not with machies.
